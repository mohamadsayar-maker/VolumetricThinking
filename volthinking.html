The shift

Computing is leaving the screen.
Design thinking has not caught up.

For decades, digital products have been shaped by the logic of pages, panels, windows, and rectangular displays.

Now software is beginning to move into rooms, objects, bodies, and physical environments. Yet many spatial products still rely on the same assumptions we learned from websites and mobile apps.

We place interfaces in three-dimensional space and call them spatial, even when nothing fundamental has changed.

A floating panel is not automatically a spatial experience.
The problem

New technology requires more than new interface patterns.

Spatial computing is often treated as an extension of screen design. Panels are moved into space. Menus are attached to objects. Familiar applications are recreated around the user.

But spatial experience design introduces different questions.

Where is the user's attention?

How does the body participate?

What does the environment contribute?

How should digital behaviour respond to movement, scale, proximity, memory, and context?

Volumetric Thinking was written to help designers reason about these questions more clearly.

This is not a book about making interfaces look three-dimensional. It is about changing how we think when the environment itself becomes part of the product.

Why now

The environment is becoming computable.

Spatial hardware is becoming more capable. AI systems are beginning to understand images, objects, rooms, movement, and intent. Digital experiences are moving beyond isolated screens and becoming embedded in the world around us.

Rooms can become interfaces.

Objects can become contextual.

Interactions can respond to place, behaviour, and change over time.

The design language for this future is still being formed. The decisions designers make now may become the conventions that shape spatial products for years to come.

The future of XR is not about wearing computers. It is about making the environment itself computable.
The design challenge

You may know how to design a product, but still feel unprepared to design a world.

Many experienced designers enter spatial projects with strong skills in product thinking, interaction design, visual systems, prototyping, and user research. But familiar tools begin to feel incomplete.

Wireframes do not describe movement through space. Page hierarchies do not capture relationships between people, objects, and environments. Linear user flows cannot always represent experiences that unfold through attention, proximity, gesture, memory, and context.

The difficulty is not a lack of design experience. It is that spatial products require a different mental model.

The idea

Volumetric Thinking is a way of seeing before it is a way of making.

It asks designers to stop treating space as a background for digital content and begin treating it as an active part of the experience. Instead of designing isolated interfaces, we design relationships.

Between the body and the environment.

Between attention and information.

Between objects and behaviour.

Between intelligence and context.

Between what is happening now and what the system remembers over time.

The book introduces six cognitive shifts that help designers move from screen-based assumptions toward spatial-native thinking.

The framework

Six shifts for designing beyond the frame

01

From surfaces to volumes

From designing bounded canvases: pages, frames, and screens.
To shaping fields of attention and interaction that surround the user.

02

From hierarchy to proximity

From importance expressed through size, contrast, and placement on a surface.
To importance expressed through distance, orientation, and reach.

03

From screens to environments

From the product as a frame the user looks at.
To the product as a system the user is inside.

04

From flow to choreography

From journeys described as screens, states, and clicks.
To journeys that unfold through the body: looking, turning, reaching, moving.

05

From interface to embodiment

From the user as an operator outside the system.
To the body as part of the system.

06

From static layout to adaptive spatial intelligence

From fixed layouts that stay the same regardless of context.
To environments that reorganise around intent and learn over time.

What you will gain

Move from placing interfaces in space to designing experiences that belong there.

By the end of the book, readers should be better equipped to:

Recognise when a spatial product is still relying on screen-based assumptions.

Decide when an experience should use space, movement, embodiment, or environmental context.

Design relationships between people, objects, information, and intelligent systems.

Evaluate concepts using spatial-native principles rather than visual novelty.

Communicate spatial ideas clearly across multidisciplinary teams.

Create experiences that feel situated, intentional, and native to the world around them.

About the author

Written from two decades of designing across screens, systems, and space.

Mo Sayyar is a product and spatial experience designer with more than 20 years of experience across digital products, XR, AI, creative tools, interaction systems, and emerging technology.

Across his work, he repeatedly encountered the same challenge. Teams had access to powerful new technologies, but often relied on inherited models to decide what to build with them. Spatial products were treated as expanded screens. Immersive interfaces repeated familiar patterns. New capabilities were introduced without a new way of thinking about the relationship between people, technology, and the environment.

Volumetric Thinking grew from that tension. It brings together lessons from product design, architecture, embodied interaction, spatial computing, and AI to help establish a more spatial-native design discipline.
